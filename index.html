01 / About

Observer.
Synthesizer.
Convener.

I started as a wayward young man and a published film critic. That background trained something useful: the habit of watching carefully, reading what's actually happening beneath the surface, and finding language that makes other people see it too.

That habit became a professional discipline. Early on — working within Sumner Redstone's top-three U.S. cinema chain — I was sitting at the table where consumer data, studio strategy, and exhibitor economics collided daily. I wasn't running analytics software. I was learning to read a market before the tools existed to formalize it. Mathematical curiosity feeding commercial instinct. Scientist mode.

At Boston Globe Media, that instinct became a system. I closed $9.8M in studio advertising in a single year — not by pitching harder, but by translating market intelligence into proposals that made the business case obvious. I built multi-year contracts, co-created a documentary film festival program, and managed relationships across every major studio and independent distributor through the major agency networks. The data was always the argument. The creativity was how you delivered it. Artist mode.

Then the market changed. It always does. At National CineMedia, I moved from publisher to vendor — selling cinema audiences at scale, managing territory budgets, and applying two decades of consumer and market intelligence to a new problem set. At Threatpost / Kaspersky Lab, I built an advertising revenue program in cybersecurity media from near-zero — growing ACV 7× by applying the same business intelligence discipline to an entirely different vertical. Scientist again.

Mar-tech has threaded through all of it — as a product I've sold, a tool I've deployed, and a capability I've built teams around. That fluency only deepens from here.

I'm 56 and still building. The next decade looks like everything the last three did: new industry context, same core practice — observe, synthesize, convene, move.

Boston Globe Media

Proposed a DVD bundling and distribution model through the Globe's subscriber network — years before streaming redefined home entertainment. The business case was built on consumer behavior data that pointed clearly toward what was coming. A studio took it seriously. The market caught up later.

2007
The Simpsons Movie — 12-Channel Campaign
Fox · Boston Globe Media

Built a 12-channel campaign around a single film release — print, digital, events, street activation, broadcast, and promotional partnerships — at a time when most studio campaigns ran two or three. The market intelligence said Boston fans would respond to immersion. They did. The Simpsonize the Sox activation alone drove measurable earned media.

2005

Paramount · Boston Globe Media

Proposed a full commuter-hub takeover at Boston's South Station before "experiential marketing" was a standard line item. 10,000 branded Globe newspapers, street teams, live activation, and a Boston.com content trail — total package value $89,000. Proved that a newspaper could own a physical space the way a billboard never could.

04 / Why It Matters

What Makes the Difference

Five industries. One consistent practice: get the data right, build the argument, bring the right people together, and move.

I.
Data That Moves People

Consumer, market, and business intelligence has been the core of every role — from reading box office patterns before analytics tools existed, to structuring studio contracts around audience data, to building a cybersecurity advertising program from scratch. Analysis without translation is just noise. The value is in the argument it enables.

II.
Three Sides of the Table

Publisher, vendor, and client-side experience across 30 years means I've been on every side of every deal I'm likely to encounter. That's not a résumé line — it's a different way of reading a room. I know what the buyer is thinking because I've been the buyer.

III.
Mar-Tech as a Native Language

CRM platforms, digital advertising operations, sales enablement tools, audience analytics — across automotive, cybersecurity, media, and entertainment. Whether it's a product I'm selling or a tool I'm running, the discipline is the same: get the data clean, build the pipeline right, and let the system do its job.

IV.
Scientist → Artist → Repeat

The rhythm runs through everything. Build the analytical case. Then find the human story inside it. Then test the story against new data. Then tell it better. This is how the $9.8M year happened. It's how a 7× ACV growth happened in a completely different vertical. The cycle doesn't stop.

V.
Still Building

Thirty years in, the instinct is sharper, not slower. New platforms, new verticals, new buyer behavior — these are interesting problems, not threats. The next decade will require the same thing the last three did: curiosity, pattern recognition, adaptation, and the willingness to go first.

VI.
The Convener

Some of the highest-value work I've done never showed up on a rate card. Pulling together editorial, sales, events, and production teams around a shared opportunity. Building the cross-functional case for a partnership nobody else saw. Creating programs — like a documentary film festival — that didn't exist before the conversation. That's leadership in practice.
